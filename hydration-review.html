Conclusions

  • Three of four picks are clean locks — cleanser, essence, ceramide cream.
  • The serum is the one conditional lock. It scores 6.8 as-written because lavender water and witch hazel water sit above all the hyaluronic acid. With those two removed (a Pravada customization), it jumps to ~8.6.
  • The runners-up only out-score the serum because they dodge that flaw — but both are off-statement (Tri-Active is clarifying, Stem-A is anti-aging). So they're not real swaps. Fix the hero; don't replace it.
  • All four pass Sephora Clean.

Which wins on hydration

The serum wins raw hydration delivery. The cream wins as the best hydration product. That distinction is the whole story of the line.

Delivery winner

Multi-Molecular HA Serum

Puts water into the skin at every depth: five molecular weights of hyaluronic acid, plus Tremella (snow mushroom, binds ~500× its weight in water) and polyglutamic acid filming on top. Nothing else in the line moves more water into more layers. If hydration means adding moisture, the serum wins — and it's the product a creator can demo on camera and a customer can feel in seconds. Wins the first purchase.

Best overall — top score, 8.9

Barrier Renewal Ceramide Cream

Does the one thing the serum can't: it holds the water in. Three ceramides plus cholesterol and phytosphingosine rebuild the skin's lipid barrier and cut moisture loss. Every humectant upstream is fighting evaporation — this is the only product that closes the door. It's also the repeat-purchase product: the one customers finish and rebuy. Wins the customer.

The cleanser and essence are genuinely hydrating too (8.4 each), but they're supporting hydration — a cleanser that doesn't strip, a water-layer that preps the skin. Neither is a reason someone buys the line. The serum and the cream are a pair — humectant delivery plus barrier seal — and that pairing is exactly why the regimen is a system, not four humectant products stacked.

Where peptides fit

Ranked by how readily each product could carry a peptide story without breaking the hydration statement — based on the real ingredient lists.

Readiest fit

Peptide+ Firming Serum — repositioned

Its ingredient list is already hydration-led — glycerin is #2, sodium hyaluronate is #4, the full amino-acid complex follows — with a five-peptide stack layered after, and no witch hazel, alcohol, or fragrance. Mechanically it's a hydration serum that happens to carry peptides. Drop the word "Firming," call it a peptide-plump / hydra-peptide serum, and it's a daily, on-label peptide product that doesn't fight the statement. This is the one to sample.

Supporting only

Barrier Renewal Ceramide Cream

Already in the line — but it carries collagen amino acids, not signal peptides. It can't honestly take a "peptide" headline, though it supports an adjacent barrier + amino-acid repair story. Useful as supporting language, not as the peptide hero.

Future statement #2

Multi-Peptide Smoothing Moisturizer

The strongest peptide-led product — full peptide stack on a real hydration base, so it would hydrate well. But it's unmistakably peptide-led by name and design: it belongs to a future Youth statement, not this hydration launch. Sample it only to feel a true peptide-led texture for planning.

Where peptides do not fit readily: the Peptide+ Hydro Gel Cleanser (peptides wash off in a rinse-off — mechanically pointless), and forcing them into the cleanser, essence, or hero serum (none carry signal peptides; reformulating for no reason).

Bottom line

On hydration, the serum and the ceramide cream are the two that win — delivery plus retention, a deliberate pair. On peptides, the Peptide+ Firming Serum slots in most readily and honestly: it's already a hydration serum with peptides, so it answers the "peptides somewhere" question without diluting the statement. The Multi-Peptide Moisturizer is the strongest true peptide product, but it's a future Statement #2 anchor — not a launch fit.
